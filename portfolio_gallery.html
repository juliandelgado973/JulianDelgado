Event Coordinator / Project Ops Lead
Fig. 01 — On-site during the launch · Barclays Center · NY Liberty, Sept 17, 2025

On the New York Liberty's first home game as defending champions, I ran the operational launch of the Toki Row luxury suite — a Suntory Toki Whiskey–branded hospitality space at the top of the Barclays bowl. I owned the run-of-show end to end, from a $20K procurement plan through a 7-hour live service window, coordinating a 7-person floor team (two of them new hires) plus contracted staff. A single pre-doors readiness gate at 6:15 PM separated setup from service, and a live ops check at halftime kept F&B stock and staffing ahead of demand. To ease arrival pressure at the suite, I also staged an outdoor fan activation — a 1v1 basketball hoop mounted to a school bus — that gave early guests something to do and smoothed the entry flow.

Wall text — Lessons learned
What this launch taught me
01
Handle a late staffing change by governance, not improvisation. When two team members left just before the launch and management stepped in as replacements, I logged the change in the issue log, followed escalation protocol, and kept the change visible rather than quietly absorbing it — so the swap was a documented decision, not a hidden risk.
02
Onboard replacements with the tools already in hand. I coached the new members through several virtual meetings and used the run-of-show itself as the training script, walking them through posts and escalation so that by 6:15 no one was guessing their role on a champion's opening night.
03
Document the risk you hope you never need. Staff attrition — for personal or professional reasons — should have been logged as a named risk with a mitigation plan from the start. On the next build, it will be.

Fig. 02 — Moroccanoil retail display · 5-shelf lit fixture · Henschel-Steinau

Led prototype development and field execution for a premium Moroccanoil retail display — a five-glass-shelf fixture with integrated shelf lighting, a metal frame, and an acrylic back wall. The work ran through 26+ client-driven revisions as budgets and stakeholder vision shifted, so the real job was absorbing change without losing the build. Most of the wins came from engineering around constraints rather than fighting them, and from treating shipping and packaging as part of the product. It ended with a clean HQ install: 45 minutes, one associate, zero defects.

Artifact B Key Engineering Decisions & QA Sign-off The fixes that made the build ship and last
Decision 01 · Lighting vs. storage

In-cabinet power box

The client wanted lit shelves and lower-cabinet storage. A power box screwed to the inner cabinet wall powered every shelf while preserving the storage space — solving both without a redesign.

Decision 02 · Finish

Brushed silver over powder coat

Powder-coated frames smudged and chipped in transit from the production vendor. A sleek straight silver metal proved far more maintainable and rarely showed damage after shipping.

Decision 03 · Freight

Sea freight over air

Switched from air to boat freight. Slightly more expensive per container, but it markedly better preserved the quality of the metal, glass, and lighting on the way to the store.

Decision 04 · Packaging

Optimized corrugated pack-outs

Re-engineered the corrugated inserts around the parts that kept arriving damaged — metal, glass, and lighting — trading a little material cost for a cleaner delivered product.

Wall text — Lessons learned
What this rollout taught me
01
Some revisions you manage, not prevent. The 26 rounds came from real client budget limits and stakeholder-vision changes — legitimate and largely unavoidable. I channeled them by offering structured feedback options, a habit from earlier projects, so each round closed as a decision instead of drifting.
02
Engineer around the constraint. Lit shelves versus storage looked like a trade-off until the in-cabinet power box delivered both. The strongest fixes reframed a "pick one" into a "keep both."
03
Quality is decided in transit, not just on the bench. Brushed silver over powder coat, sea over air freight, and re-engineered pack-outs mattered more to the delivered product than the prototype did. I learned to optimize the whole journey to the shelf.
